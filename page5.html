Project Framework

This project was inspired, in part, by the work of Dr. Robin Conley Riner. Her book, XXX, served as a critical lens for examining the ways in which the humanity of ‘capital offenders’ is intentionally narrated and mitigated throughout the legal process.

Riner argues that, through language, capital offenders are routinely dehumanized throughout the sentencing phase of capital trials. Further, Riner argues that jurors-- the people who are ultimately responsible for determining a death penalty sentence, are socialized into the legal institution in a way that both mitigates their empathy toward the defendant and distances themselves from understanding their own agency within the capital trial process. How can a human sentence another to die; and who is ultimately responsible for these “death decisions?"

“The legal institution provides jurors with the attitudes and language that enable them to restrain their empathy toward another human being, in order that they may live with the decision that someone else will die.”
(Conley 2016: 119)

This project takes up Riner’s argument that the capital trial process creates an affective and moral distance between capital offenders and the rest of society through linguistic and embodied processes that strip the capital offender of their personhood.

This project aims to close that distance, centering the humanity of those who have been executed via capital punishment and the agency of those impose death penalty decisions. 

The data, then, for this project will be examined with the following goals in mind: 

Institutional Patterns

To reestablish responsibility and agency in death penalty sentencing and executions 

Biographical Information

To center the personhood of those who were executed by the State of Texas

Last Words

To reestablish empathy for 'capital offenders' by having direct contact with their spoken words
